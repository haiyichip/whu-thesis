Marine propellers are prone to complex vibration responses during operation, and their modal characteristics directly affect propulsion performance, structural strength, and noise level. Although traditional finite-element-based modal analysis provides high accuracy, it suffers from high computational cost and low efficiency, making it difficult to meet the demand for rapid evaluation in multi-parameter design spaces. In addition, high-order wet modal tests are difficult to obtain, and the applicability of existing empirical formulas under complex operating conditions is still limited. To address these issues, this thesis proposes a wet modal frequency prediction method for marine propellers by integrating high-fidelity numerical modeling, modal testing, and machine learning.

First, in terms of numerical modeling, a high-fidelity finite element simulation method and a standardized calculation procedure for marine propellers are established. By unifying material parameters, boundary conditions, meshing strategies, and wet modal solution processes, highly consistent and reliable calculations of dry and wet modal frequencies are achieved, providing a solid basis for subsequent sample generation and frequency prediction. Second, in terms of experimental testing, a reliable modal testing method for marine propellers is developed, through which modal shape characteristics from low-order to high-order modes are obtained. The variation law of the added mass effect caused by attached water under fluid-structure coupling for different modal orders is also revealed, providing experimental support for wet modal simulation validation and empirical formula correction. On this basis, in terms of machine learning, a large-scale expansion of a multi-type propeller sample database is realized by combining parametric modeling with automated simulation workflows, and a multi-source training dataset is constructed by integrating high-fidelity simulation results, experimental data, and empirical formulas. Three machine learning algorithms, namely multilayer perceptron (MLP), XGBoost, and LightGBM, are employed to build predictive models for wet modal frequencies of propellers, and their prediction accuracy and generalization capability are comparatively evaluated.

The results show that the machine-learning-based surrogate models can effectively approximate high-fidelity finite element simulation results while significantly reducing computation time and maintaining high prediction accuracy. Among them, gradient boosting models exhibit stronger stability in predicting medium- and high-order modes, whereas neural network models show certain advantages in predicting low-order modes. The proposed method extends and corrects traditional empirical formulas and forms a modal frequency big-data model with both high accuracy and physical interpretability, providing an efficient and feasible technical route for the rapid prediction and optimal design of vibration characteristics of marine propellers.
